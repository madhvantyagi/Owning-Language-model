SFT Mathematical Foundations

A First-Principles Guide to Cross-Entropy Loss and Gradient Backpropagation

1. The Core Objective: Next-Token Prediction (Why Probability?)

Imagine you are training a model to write a sentence. How do you measure if it did a "good" job? A sentence is a sequence of tokens: $Y = (y_1, y_2, \dots, y_T)$.

The Problem: Measuring Success

If we want the model to generate the exact sequence, it must guess the first word correctly, AND guess the second word correctly given the first, AND guess the third correctly given the first two. Because language is inherently ambiguous, the model is essentially guessing. Therefore, we must operate in the realm of probability.

Using the chain rule of probability, the joint probability of generating the entire correct sequence is the product of the probabilities of each individual guess:

$$P_\theta(y_1, y_2, \dots, y_T) = \prod_{t=1}^T P_\theta(y_t \mid y_{<t})$$
Wait, what is that subscript notation ($y_{<t}$)?

In the formula above, you see $y_{<t}$ sitting down low in the subscript. In mathematics, this is a common shorthand.

  • $y_t$ means "the specific token at step $t$".
  • $y_{<t}$ means "all the tokens that came before step $t$".

So, if we are trying to predict the 4th word ($y_4$), the expression $P_\theta(y_4 \mid y_{<4})$ simply translates to: "The probability of guessing the 4th word, given words 1, 2, and 3."

Our fundamental goal is to find the parameters $\theta$ that maximize this joint probability.

2. Deriving the Cross-Entropy Loss (Why Logarithms?)

The Problem: Numerical Underflow

Suppose our sequence has 1000 tokens. We are multiplying 1000 probabilities (numbers between 0 and 1). The result will be something like $0.0000000001$. A computer's floating-point architecture cannot handle this; it will underflow and round to exactly $0$. We cannot optimize $0$!

The Insight: Turn Multiplication into Addition

How did mathematicians hundreds of years ago multiply massive numbers before calculators? They used logarithms! A fundamental property of logarithms is that $\log(a \times b) = \log(a) + \log(b)$.

The Negative Sign: Why do we negate it?

You might wonder why there is a big minus sign ($-$) at the front of the Cross-Entropy formula. It comes down to two mathematical facts:

  1. Logarithms of fractions are negative: A probability is always a number between 0 and 1. If you take the logarithm of any number between 0 and 1, the result is always negative! For example, $\log(0.1) \approx -2.3$.
  2. Optimizers minimize, they don't maximize: When we sum up all those negative log-probabilities, we get a large negative number. We want to maximize this (push it up towards 0). However, deep learning tools (like PyTorch) are designed to perform gradient descent—they want to minimize a positive "loss" down to zero.

By simply multiplying the whole sum by $-1$, we flip the negative log-probabilities into positive errors. Now, a perfect prediction (probability 1.0) has a loss of $0$, and a terrible prediction (probability 0.01) has a high positive loss. We have successfully created a minimization problem!

$$\mathcal{L}(\theta) = -\sum_{t=1}^T \log P_\theta(y_t \mid y_{<t})$$
Wait, why do we sometimes divide by $N$ (or $T$)?

In many implementations (like PyTorch's CrossEntropyLoss), you will see the formula divided by $N$ or $T$ (the total number of tokens or batch size). This turns the sum into a mean (average). Why?

  • Fairness across sequence lengths: If you sum the loss for a 1,000-word sentence, the number will be 100 times larger than a 10-word sentence. This means the gradients would be 100x larger, causing the model to take erratic, explosive steps.
  • Decoupling from Batch Size: By dividing by the number of tokens, the loss (and the resulting gradients) remains on the exact same scale regardless of whether your batch size is 1 or 128. This allows you to change your batch size without having to constantly retune your learning rate.

Information Theory Perspective: Why else use logarithms? In information theory, $-\log(P)$ measures "surprise" or "information content." If the model predicts the correct word with 100% probability ($P=1$), the surprise is $-\log(1) = 0$. If it predicts it with 1% probability ($P=0.01$), the surprise is huge. We are training the model to be unsurprised by the training data.

3. The Softmax Function (Why Exponentials?)

At any given step, the neural network outputs raw, unbounded numbers called logits (let's call them $z$) for every word in the vocabulary. Logits can range from $-\infty$ to $+\infty$. But probabilities must be between 0 and 1, and sum exactly to 1.

The Problem: Creating Probabilities

How do we map a list of arbitrary numbers to a valid probability distribution? We could just divide each logit by the sum of all logits. But what if some logits are negative? We would get negative probabilities, which makes no mathematical sense.

The Insight: The Boltzmann Distribution

We need a function that makes everything positive, no matter what. The exponential function $e^z$ does exactly this. In statistical mechanics, this is known as the Boltzmann distribution, which maps energy states to probabilities. By exponentiating the logits and dividing by the sum, we create a soft, winner-take-all probability distribution:

$$p_i = \frac{e^{z_i}}{\sum_{k=1}^V e^{z_k}}$$

4. The Calculus of Learning (Deriving the Gradient Step-by-Step)

How do we actually change the logits to minimize the loss? We need to calculate the gradient: the partial derivative of the loss with respect to each logit $z$. Let's derive it step-by-step for a single token.

Let $c$ represent the index of the correct target token. The loss $\mathcal{L}$ for this step is $-\log p_c$. If we expand $p_c$ using the Softmax definition:

$$\mathcal{L} = -\log \left( \frac{e^{z_c}}{\sum_{k=1}^V e^{z_k}} \right)$$

Using logarithm properties ($\log(A/B) = \log A - \log B$) and knowing that $\log(e^x) = x$, we simplify the loss function:

$$\mathcal{L} = -z_c + \log \left( \sum_{k=1}^V e^{z_k} \right)$$

Now, we take the partial derivative of this loss with respect to the output logits to find our gradients.

Case A: Differentiating with respect to the correct logit ($i = c$):

The derivative of the first term ($-z_c$) with respect to $z_c$ is simply $-1$. To differentiate the log-sum-exp term, we use the chain rule (derivative of $\log(u)$ is $1/u \times u'$):

$$\frac{\partial \mathcal{L}}{\partial z_c} = -1 + \left( \frac{1}{\sum_{k=1}^V e^{z_k}} \times e^{z_c} \right)$$

Notice that the second term is literally just our Softmax probability $p_c$! So it simplifies beautifully to:

$$\frac{\partial \mathcal{L}}{\partial z_c} = p_c - 1$$

Case B: Differentiating with respect to an incorrect logit ($i \neq c$):

The derivative of the first term ($-z_c$) with respect to $z_i$ is $0$. The log-sum-exp term differentiates the exact same way, pulling out $e^{z_i}$ in the numerator, which produces $p_i$:

$$\frac{\partial \mathcal{L}}{\partial z_i} = 0 + p_i = p_i$$
The Software Engineering Perspective: Vectorization

In a real neural network, doing an if/else statement for the correct vs. incorrect logits would be incredibly slow on a GPU. We need a single math equation that handles everything at once.

Enter the one-hot vector ($\mathbf{y}$). This is a vector that is exactly 1.0 at the correct token's index, and 0.0 everywhere else.

If we define our gradient as the probability vector ($\mathbf{p}$) minus the one-hot vector ($\mathbf{y}$), watch what happens magically:

  • For the correct index: The one-hot vector is $1$. The math becomes $p_c - 1$.
  • For the incorrect indices: The one-hot vector is $0$. The math becomes $p_i - 0 = p_i$.

This allows PyTorch to calculate the gradient for all 128,000 vocabulary words simultaneously in a single, lightning-fast subtraction!

Expressed as a single vector operation, the error gradient passed back to the model is simply:

$$\frac{\partial \mathcal{L}}{\partial \mathbf{z}} = \mathbf{p} - \mathbf{y}$$

5. The Training Engine: Hyperparameter Intuition

In the trainer code, you see many parameters (Batch Size, Warmup, Weight Decay). What do these actually do? Let's understand them without equations, using real-world intuition.

Batch Size & Gradient Accumulation (The Study Strategy)

Imagine reading a textbook before an exam. The Batch Size is how many pages you read before stopping to update your mental model (the weights).

  • Too small (e.g., Batch Size = 1): You react to every single sentence. If there's a typo, you learn the typo. Your learning path is erratic and noisy.
  • Too large: You read the whole book at once. It takes too much memory (VRAM), and you update your brain too infrequently, making learning slow.

Gradient Accumulation: What if you only have the brain capacity (VRAM) to read 1 page at a time, but you want the stability of reading 6 pages? You read 1 page, write down the errors on a notepad (accumulate gradients), read the next page, add those errors to the notepad, and after 6 pages, you finally update your brain. It perfectly mimics a batch size of 6, but uses 1/6th the peak memory!

Learning Rate & Warmup Ratio (The Foggy Valley)

You are blindfolded in a foggy valley, trying to reach the bottom (minimize loss). The Learning Rate is the size of the steps you take based on the slope under your feet.

Why Warmup? When you first drop a pre-trained model into a new SFT task, the loss landscape is unfamiliar. The initial gradients might be huge and erratic. If you immediately take massive steps (high learning rate), you will "break your legs"—you'll destroy the delicate knowledge the model learned during pre-training (this is called catastrophic forgetting).
Warmup means starting with tiny, microscopic steps. Over the first few steps (e.g., 3% of training), you slowly increase your stride to the maximum learning rate. You stretch before you sprint.

Weight Decay (Occam's Razor)

Imagine a spring attached to every weight in the model, constantly pulling it gently back toward zero. That is Weight Decay.

Why do this? If a model is left unchecked, it might memorize the training data by making certain weights massively large. Massive weights mean the model is highly confident but brittle (overfitting). By constantly penalizing large weights, we force the model to distribute its knowledge efficiently. It enforces simplicity—"keep your weights as close to zero as possible, unless they are absolutely necessary to solve the task."

AdamW 8-bit (The Momentum Tracker)

Standard gradient descent just looks at the slope right now. AdamW acts like a heavy boulder rolling down the hill. It remembers the direction it has been rolling (momentum) and keeps rolling that way, smoothing out potholes and noisy gradients.

Why 8-bit? Adam needs to store these "momentum" states for every single weight. This takes double the memory of the model itself! By quantizing these optimizer states to 8-bit integers, we slash memory usage without significantly harming the final accuracy.
