SFT ∵ GRPO

The actual mathematical difference — gradient geometry, probability distributions,
and what each loss does to your model's weights.

You already know the high-level story: GRPO uses a reward verifier and group-relative advantage, SFT matches a fixed target sequence. You want the mechanical difference — what changes in the loss, in the gradient, in the weight update, in the probability distribution the model is walking toward.

Here it is, from four different mathematical perspectives, no fluff.

1. Loss function mechanics

SFT — cross-entropy against a fixed external target

\[ \mathcal{L}_{\text{SFT}} = -\sum_{t}\; \log \pi_\theta(y_t^* \mid x, y_{\lt t}^*) \]

Every gradient step says "push probability mass toward this exact token, at this exact position, because the label says so." The target sequence \(y^*\) is exogenous — it came from a human or a stronger model. The current policy's opinion about whether that token was likely, hard, or even the best choice never enters the loss.

It's the same MLE objective you'd use for any classification problem, just autoregressive.

GRPO — group-relative policy gradient + KL regularization

\[ \mathcal{L}_{\text{GRPO}} \propto -\frac{1}{G}\sum_{i=1}^{G}\sum_{t} \min\!\bigl(r_t(\theta)\,A_i,\; \text{clip}(r_t(\theta),\,1-\varepsilon,\,1+\varepsilon)\,A_i\bigr) \;-\; \beta\cdot\text{KL}(\pi_\theta \parallel \pi_{\text{ref}}) \]

where \(r_t(\theta) = \frac{\pi_\theta(y_t \mid x, y_{\lt t})}{\pi_{\text{ref}}(y_t \mid x, y_{\lt t})}\) and \(A_i = \dfrac{R_i - \text{mean}(R_{\text{group}})}{\text{std}(R_{\text{group}})}\).

The critical term is \(A_i\). The sign of the gradient for every token in completion \(i\) depends on whether that completion, sampled from the model itself, scored above or below the average of other completions the model also just generated for the same prompt.

Crux: SFT's gradient direction is given. GRPO's gradient direction is computed, relative to the model's own current behavior on that exact prompt. There is no external target sequence at all.

2. Linear algebra

Same rank-1 outer product, completely different coefficient

Any gradient step on a weight matrix \(W\) — say the unembedding matrix, easiest to see — comes from backprop as an outer product:

\[ \Delta W = -\eta \cdot \sum_t \bigl( \mathbf{error}_t \otimes \mathbf{h}_t \bigr) \]

where \(\mathbf{h}_t\) is the hidden state at position \(t\) and \(\mathbf{error}_t\) is the backprop signal flowing out of the softmax at that position. This structural fact — gradients are sums of rank-1 outer products of "upstream activation" and "downstream error" — is true for SFT and GRPO alike.

There is no difference in the shape of the update. The entire difference lives in what \(\mathbf{error}_t\) equals.

SFT

\(\mathbf{error}_t = \text{softmax}(\mathbf{h}_t) - \text{onehot}(y_t^*)\)
coefficient weight = 1, always.

Every token in the dataset contributes exactly one fixed-sign, fixed-magnitude pull: rotate the row of \(W\) corresponding to the demonstrated token toward \(\mathbf{h}_t\), rotate every other row away. Deterministic. The target one-hot vector is external and fixed before you ever compute a gradient.

GRPO

\(\mathbf{error}_t = A_i \cdot \bigl(\text{softmax}(\mathbf{h}_t) - \text{onehot}(y_t)\bigr)\)
where \(y_t\) is self-generated.

Same outer-product structure — but now the "target" \(\text{onehot}(y_t)\) is self-generated, and the coefficient \(A_i\) is signed. If \(A_i \lt 0\), the update literally flips direction: it rotates the row for \(y_t\) away from \(\mathbf{h}_t\) — the model is punishing itself for producing a token it just produced.

In vector terms: SFT's gradient is a fixed vector field pointing at externally-given targets. GRPO's gradient is a signed, reweighted average over the model's own sampled directions. That reweighting is the entire trick — nothing else structurally differs.

3. Probability

What distribution is each one actually converging toward?

This is the sharper way to see "why GRPO can discover things SFT can't."

SFT — forward KL to a fixed data distribution

\[ \text{SFT:}\quad \arg\min_\theta \; \text{KL}\bigl(P_{\text{data}}(y \mid x) \;\|\; \pi_\theta(y \mid x)\bigr) \]

Forward KL is mode-covering: if \(P_{\text{data}}\) puts a point mass on one demonstrated trajectory, \(\pi_\theta\) is dragged to put mass there too, full stop, with no reference to whether \(\pi_\theta\) already "believed" something better was possible.

GRPO — Boltzmann tilting of the model's own distribution

GRPO is a score-function (REINFORCE) estimator of the gradient of expected reward, KL-regularized to a reference policy. The actual fixed point — the distribution \(\pi^*\) that maximizes \(\mathbb{E}[R] - \beta\cdot\text{KL}(\pi\|\pi_{\text{ref}})\) — has a closed form, well known from control-as-inference:

\[ \pi^*(y \mid x) \;\propto\; \pi_{\text{ref}}(y \mid x) \;\cdot\; \exp\!\bigl(R(y) / \beta\bigr) \]

That's a Boltzmann / Gibbs tilting of the reference distribution by reward — not a point mass on any specific demonstrated sequence. GRPO is trying to sculpt the model's own distribution so that probability mass gets exponentially reallocated toward whatever the model already assigns even slightly-higher-than-average reward to. It's mode-seeking w.r.t. reward, not mode-covering w.r.t. some external trace.

This is the deep reason SFT can't produce emergent behavior (long backtracking chains, adaptive reasoning length) and GRPO can: SFT's target distribution is fixed in advance by a human/model transcript.
GRPO's target distribution is defined self-referentially in terms of the model's own reward-weighted output distribution. Whatever weird trajectory the model stumbles into that scores well gets exponentially amplified — there's no requirement that trajectory ever existed in any dataset.

4. Why group-relative normalization isn't a hack

It's exploiting a zero-mean identity

The advantage baseline — subtracting the group mean — looks arbitrary but it's actually free. The score function has a zero-mean property under its own distribution:

\[ \mathbb{E}_{y \sim \pi_\theta}\bigl[\nabla_\theta \log \pi_\theta(y)\bigr] = \nabla_\theta \sum_y \pi_\theta(y) = \nabla_\theta (1) = 0 \]

So for any constant \(b(x)\) that doesn't depend on \(y\):

\[ \mathbb{E}_y\bigl[b \cdot \nabla_\theta \log \pi_\theta(y)\bigr] = b \cdot 0 = 0 \]

Meaning replacing \(R(y)\) with \(R(y) - b\) in the policy gradient changes nothing in expectation — same true gradient direction — but changes the variance a lot. GRPO subtracting the group mean and dividing by group std is just picking the \(b\) that minimizes estimator variance, for free, with zero bias cost.

There's no equivalent concept in SFT, because SFT was never estimating an expectation over a stochastic policy in the first place — it's plain MLE over fixed data. There's no "variance of the gradient estimator" to reduce.

5. Why GRPO "works better" for reasoning

It's not that one is a strictly stronger algorithm. They're optimizing fundamentally different quantities:

SFT

Minimizes distance to a reference trajectory.

GRPO

Maximizes probability of self-generated trajectories that a verifier confirms are correct, relative to the model's own alternatives.

For tasks where being correct at the end is what matters and there are many valid roads there, optimizing the second objective directly is just the right objective — SFT is a proxy for it (mimic something that was presumably correct) and GRPO is the real thing (was it actually correct, whatever path you took).

That's the whole reason DeepSeek-R1-Zero could get emergent long CoT / self-correction with basically no SFT at all — those behaviors were never demonstrated, they were discovered by the model because they correlated with higher reward across its own sampled rollouts, and GRPO's loss has a mechanism to reinforce exactly that correlation.

SFT's loss structurally cannot reinforce "a behavior that correlates with success" — it can only reinforce "matches this string."

6. One-line summary that ties it together

SFT

Walks the model's parameters toward a fixed, externally-specified point mass, via unbiased-but-inflexible cross-entropy gradients.

GRPO

Walks the model's parameters toward its own reward-exponentially-tilted distribution, via a high-variance-but-unbiased Monte Carlo estimator of the true policy gradient, using the fact that \(\nabla\log\pi\) integrates to zero under its own measure to de-noise that estimator for free.

Same outer-product machinery. Completely different target.
